    The Holocaust (from the Greek  (): holos, "completely" and kaustos, "burnt"), also known as  (Hebrew: ), Churben (Yiddish: ), is the term generally used to describe the killing of approximately six million European Jews during World War II, as part of a program of deliberate extermination planned and executed by the National Socialist (Nazi) regime in Germany led by Adolf Hitler.Niewyk, Donald L. The Columbia Guide to the Holocaust, Columbia University Press, 2000, p.45: "The Holocaust is commonly defined as the murder of more than 5,000,000 Jews by the Germans in World War II." Also see "The Holocaust," Encyclopaedia Britannica, 2007: "the systematic state-sponsored killing of six million Jewish men, women and children, and millions of others, by Nazi Germany and its collaborators during World War II. The Germans called this "the final solution to the Jewish question." Other groups were persecuted and killed by the regime, including the Roma; Soviets, particularly prisoners of war; ethnic Poles; other Slavic people; the disabled; gay men; and political and religious dissidents, such as Jehovah's witnesses.Berenbaum, Michael. The World Must Know, The United States Holocaust Memorial Museum, pp.125ff."Non-Jewish victims of Nazism," Encyclopaedia Britannica. Many scholars do not include these groups in the definition of the Holocaust, defining it as the genocide of the Jews, The persecution and genocide were accomplished in stages.
